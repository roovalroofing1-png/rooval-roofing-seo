What Wind Damage Looks Like on a Utah Roof

Wind rarely announces itself with shingles in the yard. More often it leaves quieter marks that only show up when someone walks the roof.

Creased shingles

The wind lifts a shingle, folds it back, and lets it drop. What's left is a faint horizontal fold line — and underneath it, a broken seal and often a cracked mat. The shingle looks like it's "back in place," but it's loose, and the next gusty day it goes for good.

Lifted tabs and broken seal strips

Even without a visible crease, gusts can pop the adhesive strip that bonds each shingle to the one below. A shingle with a broken seal flaps in every wind afterward and lets wind-driven rain push underneath. We find these by gently checking bond lines by hand.

Missing or torn shingles

Ridges, rakes, edges, and corners take the highest uplift forces, so that's where shingles tear away first. A few missing tabs at an edge often means a wider band of broken seals nearby.

Exposed underlayment or decking

Bare felt or wood showing through means your roof's waterproofing is down to its last layer — that's urgent. Call us at (385) 424-8810 and we'll get eyes on it fast.

Lifted flashing and debris marks

Big wind pries flashing away from chimneys, valleys, and vents, and hurls branches and gravel that leave gouges and scrapes. Those scattered, linear marks look different from hail's circular pattern — if you're seeing round dark spots instead, start with our hail damage page.

Utah's Canyon Winds Are Different

The Wasatch Front gets downslope windstorms: cold air pushed over the mountains accelerates as it pours down through gaps like Weber and Parleys canyons and slams into the valley below. These events cluster in fall and winter, hit hard and locally, and need no rain or hail to wreck a roof.

The benchmark is September 8, 2020 — NWS-verified gusts of 99 mph in Farmington and 91 mph in Salt Lake City, with sustained 70–80 mph winds across Davis, Weber, Box Elder, and Cache counties. That was an extreme event, not an annual routine, but smaller downslope blows happen most years. NOAA's NCEI Storm Events Database is the public record of wind events by county and date.

Wind Damage FAQs

How windy does it have to be to damage a roof?

There's no single number. It depends on the shingles' age, how well the seal strips have held, and how exposed your roof is — edges, ridges, and corners fail first. Newer, well-sealed roofs shrug off gusts that strip an aging roof bare, and damaging gusts happen at speeds well below the 99 mph Utah saw in 2020.

My shingles look fine from the ground — could there still be damage?

Yes. A creased shingle usually settles back into place, and a broken seal strip is invisible from the driveway. Both leave the shingle loose and ready to fail in the next blow, which is why we check bond lines by hand on every slope.

What should I do first if shingles blew off?

Stay off the roof — wet decking and loose shingles are genuinely dangerous — and keep clear of any hanging branches or debris. Then book a free inspection. If you can see bare wood or underlayment, call us right away at (385) 424-8810 so it doesn't sit open to the weather.

Is the wind-damage inspection free?

Yes. We don't charge for wind inspections and there's no obligation attached. If your shingles all held, that's exactly what the report will say.

Can you repair just the damaged section?

Usually, yes. If the damage is confined to one slope or edge, we replace what's damaged and re-seal around it, matching the shingle as close as we can get — exact matches depend on what's still made in your color. If seals are broken across the whole roof, we'll tell you straight that spot repairs won't hold.
