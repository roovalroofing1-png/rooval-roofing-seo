⛈️Storm, Leaks & Damage

What should I do right after a storm?
Start with safety, not the roof. Stay away from downed power lines and call your utility or 911, get out immediately if you smell gas, and treat a sagging ceiling or water near light fixtures and outlets as urgent — the roof itself can almost always wait an hour. Inside, catch water with buckets and move anything valuable out of the way. Then photograph what you can see from the ground: ceiling stains, dented gutters, shingle pieces in the yard. Stay off the roof yourself, because wet shingles are slick. If you can see exposed wood or underlayment from the ground, call us at (385) 424-8810 so it doesn't sit open to the weather. Otherwise book an inspection and we'll assess the damage, tarp what needs it, and document what we find.
How do I tell if my roof is leaking, and where from?
Look for tan or brown rings on the ceiling or high on a wall, bubbling paint, a musty smell upstairs, or soft drywall — especially right after a storm. But the wet spot rarely sits right under the actual entry point, because water travels along the rafters and roof deck before it drips through. We check the attic and trace it back to the real source — often a valley, chimney flashing, a pipe boot, or storm-damaged shingles — rather than guessing from the ceiling stain.
What does hail and wind damage look like?
Hail shows up on shingles as bruises and soft spots, dark patches of granule loss, or a cracked shingle mat underneath — and you usually can't see any of it from the ground. Dented gutters, downspouts, and vent caps are a more reliable clue, since metal dents on impact and doesn't weather back the way shingles do. Loose granules piling up in your gutters after a storm are worth a closer look. Wind damage is usually more obvious: lifted, creased, or missing shingles, especially along ridges and edges. Either way, the only way to know for sure is to have someone get up on the roof and check.
Do I need a repair or a full replacement?
It depends on how widespread the damage is and how old the roof is. A few damaged shingles or one isolated leak is usually a repair — or a roof tune-up if there are several small issues in different spots. Replacement makes more sense when the damage is widespread, the shingles are brittle enough to crack, you're seeing curling across the whole roof or heavy granule loss in the gutters, or leaks keep coming back — especially on a roof over 20 years old. If your roof is under about 15 years old and the damage is localized, a targeted repair is usually the smarter spend. An inspection settles it before you spend anything.

🏠Materials & Options

What brand of shingles do you install?

For most Utah homes we use their architectural asphalt shingles, which hold up well to the high-altitude UV, wind, and freeze-thaw swings we get here, and they come backed by an Owens Corning manufacturer warranty on the materials — separate from our own workmanship warranty on the installation.
What's the best roofing material for Utah?
For most Utah homes, architectural asphalt shingles are the best all-around choice. They're a thicker, heavier upgrade over old 3-tab shingles and handle high-altitude UV, freeze-thaw cycles, and canyon-mouth wind far better — roughly 22 to 28 years of life versus 15 to 20 for 3-tab. Utah's intense sun and big day-to-night temperature swings do gradually dry out asphalt over the years, especially on south- and west-facing slopes, so watch for curling edges, cracking, and heavy granule loss. If you're staying 15+ years or live at elevation with heavy snow and wind, metal is worth the higher upfront cost. Tile and flat/TPO membrane fit specific situations. We'll recommend the right fit for your house during the inspection.
Metal vs. asphalt shingles — which should I pick?
It depends on how long you're staying and your budget. Pick asphalt if you'll move within 5-10 years, budget is tight, or your HOA restricts metal — architectural shingles resist most Utah hail well. Pick metal if you're staying 15+ years or live somewhere with heavy snow and wind, since it sheds snow, handles 70-80 mph canyon gusts, and lasts 40-60 years versus 22-28 for architectural asphalt, at roughly two to three times the upfront cost.
How long does a roof last in Utah?
It depends on the material: 3-tab asphalt shingles last 15-20 years, architectural asphalt shingles 22-28 years, metal roofing 40-60 years, tile 35-50 years, and flat/TPO membrane 20-25 years. Utah's freeze-thaw cycles, high-altitude UV, canyon winds, and hail all shorten these numbers if a roof isn't properly installed or maintained. Regular maintenance can add years to any of these lifespans.
What is TPO, and is it right for a flat roof?
TPO (thermoplastic polyolefin) is a single-ply roofing membrane — a wide, flexible white sheet with seams that are heat-welded rather than glued or taped. Its reflective surface cuts cooling loads and slows UV damage, and the welded seams hold up well through Utah's freeze-thaw cycles, making it a strong fit for flat or low-slope sections, additions, and garage or patio roofs too shallow for shingles. Properly installed TPO typically lasts 20-25 years in Utah's climate.
