💰Cost & Financing

How much does a new roof cost in Utah?
Most full roof replacements on a typical Utah home run about $20,000 to $30,000. Where you land in that range depends on size, pitch, and the materials you choose. We give you a firm number after an on-site inspection, not a guess over the phone.
Why do roofing quotes vary so much?
Roofing quotes vary because two houses that look the same size on paper can be very different jobs underneath. Roof pitch, accessibility, how many layers need to be torn off, decking condition, and ventilation work all change the price, so each quote is custom to your roof rather than a flat rate. That's why an on-site inspection beats comparing numbers blind.

⛈️Leaks, Hail & Wind Damage

How do I tell if my roof is leaking, and where from?
Look for tan or brown rings on the ceiling or high on a wall, bubbling paint, a musty smell upstairs, or soft drywall — especially right after a storm. But the wet spot rarely sits right under the actual entry point, because water travels along the rafters and roof deck before it drips through. We check the attic and trace it back to the real source — often a valley, chimney flashing, a pipe boot, or storm-damaged shingles — rather than guessing from the ceiling stain.
What does hail damage look like?
On shingles it shows up as bruises and soft spots, dark patches of granule loss, or a cracked shingle mat underneath — and you usually can't see it from the ground at all. Dented gutters, downspouts, and vent caps are a more reliable clue, since metal dents on impact and doesn't weather back the way shingles do. If you find loose granules piling up in your gutters after a storm, that's worth a closer look. The only way to know for sure is to have someone get up on the roof and check.
My shingles blew off in a windstorm — what do I do?
Stay off the roof — wet decking and loose shingles are genuinely dangerous, and so is any hanging debris nearby. If you can see exposed wood or underlayment from the ground, call us right away at (385) 424-8810 so it doesn't sit open to the weather. Otherwise, book an inspection and we'll assess the damage, tarp what needs it, and document what we find.
Is my roof damage an emergency or can it wait?
Roof damage itself almost never is — the roof can wait an hour. The real emergencies are downed power lines (stay away, call your utility or 911), a tree on the roof or a sagging ceiling, water near light fixtures or outlets, or any gas smell, which means evacuate immediately. Once those are handled, a leak or missing shingles can safely wait for a scheduled inspection.
What should I do first after a storm, before calling anyone?
First make sure you're safe — clear of downed lines, sagging ceilings, or gas odors. Then stop water intrusion inside with buckets or towels and move anything valuable out of the way. Photograph the damage: interior stains, dented gutters, and shingle pieces in the yard. Stay off the roof yourself — wet asphalt shingles are slick — and let a professional handle the tarping and inspection.
Can my roof be repaired or does it need replacing?
It depends on how widespread the damage is. A few damaged shingles or one isolated leak can usually be handled with a repair, or a roof tune-up if there are several small issues in different spots. Widespread granule loss, shingles so brittle they crack, or a pattern of repeat leaks means replacement makes more sense. An inspection settles it.
What are the signs my roof needs replacing?
Curling across the whole roof, heavy granule loss in the gutters, or a sagging deck usually point to replacement, especially on a roof over 20 years old. A roof with damage confined to one area and shingles that still have life left in them is more often a repair. We'll give you a straight answer after an inspection.

🏠Materials & Options

What brand of shingles do you install?

For most Utah homes we use their architectural asphalt shingles, which hold up well to the high-altitude UV, wind, and freeze-thaw swings we get here, and they come backed by an Owens Corning manufacturer warranty on the materials — separate from our own workmanship warranty on the installation.
What's the best roofing material for Utah?
For most Utah homes, architectural asphalt shingles are the best all-around choice — they handle high-altitude UV and freeze-thaw cycles far better than older 3-tab shingles and carry wind ratings suited to bench and canyon-mouth neighborhoods. If you're staying in the home 15+ years, live at elevation with heavy snow or wind, or want the lowest lifetime cost, metal roofing is worth the higher upfront price. Tile and TPO membrane fit specific situations, like steep architectural styles or flat and low-slope sections. We'll recommend the right fit for your house during an inspection.
Metal vs. asphalt shingles — which should I pick?
It depends on how long you're staying and your budget. Pick asphalt if you'll move within 5-10 years, budget is tight, or your HOA restricts metal — architectural shingles resist most Utah hail well. Pick metal if you're staying 15+ years or live somewhere with heavy snow and wind, since it sheds snow, handles 70-80 mph canyon gusts, and lasts 40-60 years versus 22-28 for architectural asphalt, at roughly two to three times the upfront cost. The right roof depends on your house, your budget, and how long you plan to live under it.
How long does a roof last in Utah?
It depends on the material: 3-tab asphalt shingles last 15-20 years, architectural asphalt shingles 22-28 years, metal roofing 40-60 years, tile 35-50 years, and flat/TPO membrane 20-25 years. Utah's freeze-thaw cycles, high-altitude UV, canyon winds, and hail all shorten these numbers if a roof isn't properly installed or maintained. South- and west-facing slopes typically wear faster from afternoon sun. Regular maintenance can add years to any of these lifespans.
What is TPO, and is it right for a flat roof?
TPO (thermoplastic polyolefin) is a single-ply roofing membrane — a wide, flexible white sheet with seams that are heat-welded rather than glued or taped. Its reflective surface cuts cooling loads and slows UV damage, and the welded seams hold up well through Utah's freeze-thaw cycles, making it a strong fit for flat or low-slope sections, additions, and garage or patio roofs too shallow for shingles. Properly installed TPO typically lasts 20-25 years in Utah's climate. For most Utah buildings, it wins on reflectivity, seam strength, and price compared to other flat-roof membranes.
Does Utah's heat and sun really damage shingles?
Not on their own — one heatwave won't destroy a healthy, properly installed asphalt roof. But repeated high temperatures, strong high-elevation sunlight, and big day-to-night swings add to the normal weathering every shingle goes through, gradually drying out the asphalt and reducing its flexibility over the years. Watch for curling or lifted edges, cracked or brittle shingles, heavy granule loss, and uneven wear on south- and west-facing slopes. If you see any of those, it's worth having someone look at the actual cause.
Architectural vs. 3-tab shingles — what's the difference?
Architectural shingles are a thicker, heavier upgrade over old-style 3-tab shingles, and it shows in how they perform in Utah — they handle high-altitude UV and freeze-thaw cycles far better and carry wind ratings suited to bench and canyon-mouth neighborhoods. That durability difference shows up in lifespan too: 15-20 years for 3-tab versus 22-28 for architectural. Architectural asphalt is the value pick for most Utah homes and what most of our replacements use.
