This page is a plain-English read of NOAA's official storm records for the two counties Rooval Roofing serves most — Salt Lake and Utah — covering 2016 through June 2026. We put it together because homeowners keep asking us a fair question: has it actually stormed hard enough around here to hurt a roof? Here are the real counts. Across those ten-plus years, NOAA logged 227 damaging-wind events (≥58 mph) and 21 significant-hail events (≥1 inch) in Salt Lake and Utah counties combined.

A quick note on the numbers. NOAA records storm events by county, not by city, so a report tied to "Salt Lake County" can mean anywhere in the county. The thresholds used here are ≥1.00 inch for significant hail and ≥50 knots (about 58 mph) for damaging wind. And 2026 is year-to-date: the data file was compiled June 25, 2026 — before Utah's summer monsoon season — so the 2026 counts are low or zero and will rise as the year gets logged. Every figure on this page comes from the NOAA NCEI Storm Events Database, per NOAA through June 2026.

Read down the columns and a pattern shows up. Damaging wind climbed sharply over the decade — from 14 events in 2016 to a peak of 58 in 2023 — while significant hail is rarer and spikier, arriving in bursts rather than every year. The biggest hail year by far was 2024, with 8 significant-hail events, the most in the ten-year window.

Salt Lake County logs roughly twice the events of Utah County over the period — partly a matter of land area, partly reporting density. But the bigger takeaway holds in both counties: damaging wind shows up far more often than significant hail.

The record-setting storms — and the full count

A few individual storms stand out in the decade of records.

  • Largest hail — 1.75 in: recorded twice, in Salt Lake County on June 13, 2016, and in Utah County on August 13, 2024 (roughly golf-ball size).
  • Strongest wind gust — 83 kt (~96 mph): Salt Lake County, July 13, 2024.

The counts above track only events that crossed the damage thresholds. Counting storms of every size, NOAA logged 37 hail events and 229 thunderstorm-wind events across the two counties in the period — totals that include the 21 significant-hail and 227 damaging-wind events above, plus many smaller ones that still wear on aging shingles over time.

What this data means for your roof

A roof doesn't need a record-breaking storm to lose granules or lift a few shingles. NOAA logged 227 damaging-wind events across these two counties in a single decade, and this kind of damage is often hard to spot from the ground.
