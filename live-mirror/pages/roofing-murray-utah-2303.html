What Murray Roofs Are Up Against

Murray's weather is easier on roofs than the high benches, but it has its own wear pattern, and knowing it is half of doing the job well.

Canyon-mouth wind. The city catches the gusts that spill out of the Wasatch canyons to the east, and those bursts can reach damaging speeds without much warning. They tend to find the weak spots first — lifting the edges of older three-tab shingles and peeling back flashing that has already gone brittle.

Freeze-thaw and ice dams. Valley-floor snow does not always melt in an orderly way. On the low-slope sections and complex rooflines common in Murray's older neighborhoods, meltwater refreezes at the eaves, backs up under the shingles, and works its way into the deck. Sound underlayment and proper attic ventilation are what keep that cycle from reaching your ceiling.

High-UV summer aging. At better than four thousand feet, the summer sun is punishing. It dries out asphalt shingles, curls their edges, and quietly bakes years off a roof's life — which is why so many Murray roofs give out earlier than their original label promised.

Housing age. A large share of Murray's homes went up between the smelter years and the postwar boom, and many still wear roofs installed over builder-grade decking. Mature trees drop debris into the gutters, and the seams and penetrations on these older roofs need a closer eye. We check the wood underneath, not just the surface, and tell you exactly what we find.
