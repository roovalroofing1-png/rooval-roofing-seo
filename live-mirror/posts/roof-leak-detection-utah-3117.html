Most roof leaks give you weeks or months of warning before water ever drips from a ceiling. The early signs are small: a faint tan ring on the drywall, a musty smell upstairs, a damp patch of attic insulation after a storm. If you know where to look, you can catch a leak while it is still a modest repair instead of a drywall-and-insulation project.

We repair roofs across Utah County and the Salt Lake Valley from our home base in Lehi, and the expensive leaks we see are almost never the sudden ones. They are the slow ones nobody checked on. Here is how to spot a leak early, where leaks usually start, and what to do once you find one.

The Early Warning Signs Inside Your House

You do not need to go outside to catch most leaks. Watch for these indoors:

  • Ceiling stains. Tan or brown rings on the ceiling or high on a wall, especially ones that darken or grow after a storm. Even a faded old stain means water got in at least once.
  • Bubbling or peeling paint. Moisture working through drywall pushes paint away from the surface before you ever see a drip.
  • A musty smell. A persistent damp smell in an upstairs room, closet, or the attic often shows up before any visible stain.
  • Warped or soft drywall. Press gently near a suspect spot. Soft drywall means water has been there a while.
  • Drips during or right after storms. A leak that only shows in heavy rain is still a leak, not a fluke.
  • Stains around a light fixture or bath fan. Treat these as urgent. Water and electrical do not mix, so switch off that circuit and make the call the same day.

A stain is not automatically the roof. Plumbing lines, bathroom vents, and plain condensation cause their share. But if a stain shows up after wind, hail, or heavy rain, the roof is the first suspect to rule out.

How to Check Your Attic After a Storm

Ten minutes with a flashlight after a heavy storm tells you more than a month of staring at ceilings. Look for:

  • Dark streaks or water trails on the underside of the roof deck
  • Wet or discolored wood around anything that pokes through the roof: chimney, plumbing pipes, vents, skylights
  • Damp, matted, or discolored insulation
  • Daylight showing through the deck where it should not
  • Rusty nail tips or winter frost on nails, which can point to condensation rather than a leak

One thing to understand: water travels. It runs along rafters and the deck, sometimes for several feet, before it drops, so the wet spot rarely sits directly under the entry point. Note where you found moisture and let the inspection trace it uphill from there.

Where Roof Leaks Actually Start

Leaks rarely begin in the middle of an open field of shingles. They start where things meet, bend, or poke through.

Valleys

The channels where two roof slopes meet carry more water than anywhere else on the roof. A worn, rusted, or poorly detailed valley is one of the most common entry points we find.

Flashing

The metal where the roof meets a chimney, a sidewall, or a dormer does more waterproofing work than the shingles around it. Age, movement, and old caulk-only repairs pull flashing loose a little at a time.

Penetrations

Every pipe, vent, and skylight is a hole in your roof with a seal around it. Rubber pipe boots crack in Utah's sun long before the shingles wear out, and a cracked boot is one of the cheapest, most common repairs we make.

Storm damage and gutters

Canyon wind creases and lifts shingles, and a lifted shingle lets wind-driven rain underneath. Our wind damage guide covers what that looks like. Hail bruising opens paths for water that often do not leak until months later. And clogged gutters back water up under the bottom courses of shingle, which is how ice dams do their damage in winter. When a Leak Is Urgent

Call the same day if water is actively dripping, if a ceiling is bulging or sagging, if water is anywhere near a light fixture or outlet, or if a stain is visibly spreading. While you wait: put a bucket under the drip, move furniture and electronics out of the way, and if water is near anything electrical, switch off that circuit at the breaker. A slow, stable stain buys you a little time, but think days, not months. Water damage compounds quietly.

Repair or Replace?

Most leaks are repairs. A cracked pipe boot, a run of loose flashing, or a handful of damaged shingles does not condemn a roof, and an honest roof repair handles it for a fraction of the cost of anything bigger.

The replacement conversation starts when the leaks keep coming: repeated leaks in different spots, widespread curling or granule loss, several slopes showing their age, or a roof that is simply at the end of its life. Frequently asked questions

How do I find where my roof is leaking from?

Start in the attic with a flashlight during or right after rain and follow the water trail uphill along the rafters or roof deck. The entry point is often several feet from the ceiling stain, because water travels before it drops. Valleys, flashing, and pipe boots are the most common sources, and a professional inspection can trace the path when the trail is not obvious.

Is a ceiling stain always a roof leak?

No. Plumbing lines, bathroom vent condensation, and attic moisture can all stain a ceiling. The tell is timing: a stain that appears or grows after wind, hail, or heavy rain points to the roof, while one that changes with shower use points to plumbing. An inspection of the roof and attic sorts out which one you have.

Can I patch a roof leak myself?

Containing the water inside, yes. Fixing the roof, we would talk you out of it. Hardware-store caulk on shingles usually masks the entry point without stopping the water, makes the real source harder to trace, and puts you on a roof you probably should not be walking. A small repair done right is the inexpensive version of this problem.

When is a roof leak an emergency?

When water is actively dripping, a ceiling is bulging, or moisture is near a light fixture or outlet, treat it as same-day urgent and switch off the affected circuit. A small, stable stain with no active drip can wait a few days for an inspection, but not months, because water damage spreads quietly between storms.
