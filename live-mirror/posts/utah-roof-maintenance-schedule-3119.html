A Utah roof does best on a simple rhythm: a homeowner walk-around each spring after the snow melts, a quick check each summer before monsoon season, a real maintenance push each fall before the first freeze, and ground-level watching through winter. Add one professional inspection a year, plus a look after any serious wind or hail, and very little on your roof will ever surprise you. Almost all of it happens from the ground, and none of it requires you on a ladder.

Between high-elevation sun, canyon wind, monsoon thunderstorms, and months of freeze-thaw, a roof here works harder than the same roof would in most states. Spring: See What Winter Did

Once the snow is off the roof, winter's bill comes due. Freeze-thaw pries at every weak spot all season, and spring is when the results show.

Your part, from the ground and inside:

  • Walk every room upstairs and check ceilings for new stains, especially after the big thaws
  • Take a flashlight into the attic and look for water trails, damp insulation, or daylight through the deck
  • Scan the roof from the yard with binoculars or your phone camera zoomed in, looking for missing, creased, or lifted shingles from winter winds
  • Check gutters for sagging, separation, and a heavy wash of granule grit
  • Make sure downspouts still drain away from the foundation

The professional part: spring is the best time for your annual inspection, while winter's damage is fresh and before summer heat bakes it in. A free roof inspection with photos gives you a dated record of the roof's condition to start the year.

Summer: Get Ahead of Monsoon Season

Early summer is dry and predictable. August is not. The goal this season is to have the small stuff fixed before the monsoon winds arrive to test it.

Your part:

  • Heat makes existing curling and cracking easier to spot, so do another slow scan from the yard
  • Glance at the sealant around visible vents and flashing with your camera zoomed in, looking for cracks or separation
  • Notice how the upstairs feels. Rooms that will not cool down and an attic that stays hot after sunset are worth mentioning at your next inspection
  • After any hailstorm, check the soft metals: dimples on gutters and downspouts, dinged window screens, flattened fins on the AC unit. Dented metal means the shingles above took the same hits
  • Trim branches back from the roofline before the wind does it for you

The professional part: a roof tune-up in early summer reseals exposed nails, replaces cracked pipe boots, and tightens up the small stuff that August storms exploit.

Fall: The Season Not to Skip

If you only do one season of maintenance a year, make it this one. Everything that goes wrong on a Utah roof in January traces back to something small that was fixable in October.

Your part:

  • Get the gutters cleaned and flowing before the first freeze, because clogged gutters are how ice dams start. If ladder work is not your thing, our gutter services crew handles it
  • Confirm downspouts are attached and draining away from the house
  • Do one more ground scan after the first big fall windstorm
  • In the attic, make sure stored boxes are not blocking the soffit vents, and look again for daylight or damp spots

The professional part: schedule any repairs now. A loose shingle in October is a quick roof repair. The same shingle in January is a leak, a stained ceiling, and wet insulation, fixed in worse weather at a worse time.

Winter: Eyes Only

Nobody belongs on a snowy or icy roof, full stop. Winter maintenance is watching, from the ground and from inside:

  • Heavy icicle walls and a ridge of ice at the eaves are ice dam warnings, not decorations
  • Uneven snowmelt, like bare stripes on an otherwise snowy roof, can hint at heat escaping the attic
  • During cold snaps, check the attic for frost on nail tips, a sign of moisture riding the house air into the attic
  • After every thaw, walk the upstairs rooms and look at the ceilings

If a leak shows up mid-winter, do not wait for spring. Winter repairs happen all season in dry weather windows, and waiting only lets the water spread.

What Is DIY and What Is Professional?

The dividing line is simple: if it needs eyes, it is yours. If it needs a ladder or tools, it is ours.

Homeowner work: ground scans with binoculars or a zoomed phone camera, attic checks with a flashlight, watching how gutters behave in rain, and dated photos of anything that looks off. That last habit is worth more than most people realize, because a photo trail of your roof's condition makes every future decision easier.

Professional work: anything on the roof itself. Walking the slopes, every repair, sealant and flashing work, snow and ice dam problems, and the annual inspection. This is not gatekeeping. Falls are the real cost, and steep Utah pitches with a two-story drop are not the place to learn ladder skills.

How Often Should a Professional Look at It?

Once a year for most roofs, plus after any hailstorm or serious windstorm. Roofs past their mid-teens, and roofs carrying old repairs, earn closer attention. Frequently asked questions

How often should a roof be inspected in Utah?

Once a year for most homes, plus after any significant hail or windstorm. Spring works especially well because it catches freeze-thaw damage from winter before summer heat and monsoon storms test the roof. Older roofs and roofs with previous repairs benefit from a closer watch.

Can I do my own roof maintenance?

The looking part, yes: ground scans with binoculars, attic checks with a flashlight, watching gutters in the rain, and taking dated photos of anything odd. The ladder-and-tools part, no. Walking a roof safely takes experience and the right equipment, and a fall costs far more than any inspection or repair ever will.

What does a roof tune-up include?

A tune-up is a small-repairs visit: resealing exposed nail heads, replacing cracked pipe boots, securing loose shingles and flashing, and touching up failing sealant. The point is to fix the little things before wind, rain, and freeze-thaw turn them into leaks, and it is the cheapest roof work you will ever pay for.

Do gutters really need cleaning every fall?

Yes, before the first freeze, and again in spring if you have trees overhead. Clogged gutters hold water and debris against the roof edge, and in winter that standing water becomes an ice dam that pushes meltwater up under the shingles. Fall gutter cleaning is the single cheapest ice dam prevention there is.
