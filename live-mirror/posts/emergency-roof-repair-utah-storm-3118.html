If wind or hail just hit your roof, here is the order of operations for the first 24 hours: make sure nobody is near downed power lines or a sagging ceiling, contain any water getting inside, photograph everything before you clean up or fix anything, and stay off the roof, because emergency tarping is ladder work for a professional. Do those four things and you have protected your family, your house, and your options.

Hour One: Safety Before the Roof

The roof can wait an hour. These cannot:

  • Downed or sagging power lines. Stay far away, keep kids and pets inside, and call your utility or 911. Never touch anything a line is touching.
  • A tree or large limb on the roof. Stay out of the rooms underneath it until someone qualified has looked at the structure.
  • Water near a light fixture or outlet. Switch off that circuit at the breaker before you put a bucket under anything.
  • A bulging or sagging ceiling. That is water collecting above the drywall. Do not stand under it.
  • The smell of gas. Get everyone out first, then call from outside.

No roof is worth an emergency room visit. Slow down and clear this list before you think about shingles.

Contain the Water Inside

If water is coming in, your job tonight is containment, not repair. Buckets and towels under active drips. Furniture, rugs, and electronics moved out of the wet zone. A plastic sheet or tarp over anything too heavy to move. If a paint blister on the ceiling is holding water, many homeowners relieve it with a small hole and a bucket underneath, which beats letting the whole patch collapse. But if the bulge is near a light fixture, kill the power to that circuit first or leave it alone entirely.

Run fans on the wet areas once the dripping slows. And keep the receipts for anything you buy tonight, tarps, fans, buckets, all of it. If you end up filing an insurance claim, your reasonable emergency costs belong in the file too.

Photograph Everything Before You Touch It

Fresh, dated phone photos are the most valuable thing you can create in the first 24 hours. Before you clean up, capture:

  • Every interior stain, drip, and wet spot, with something for scale
  • Hailstones next to a coin or tape measure, on a hard surface, before they melt
  • Dented gutters, downspouts, window screens, and the fins on your AC unit
  • Shingles or shingle pieces in the yard. Do not throw them away.
  • Wide shots of the whole house from each side
  • A note of the storm's date and rough time

Photos taken the day of the storm are worth far more than anything reconstructed from memory later, whatever you eventually decide to do. Should You Tarp the Roof Yourself? Usually Not.

Here is the honest math. Wet asphalt shingles are slick, the wind is often still gusting behind the storm, it is frequently getting dark, and the steep pitches common on newer Utah homes are hard to walk in perfect conditions. A fall costs more than any leak ever will. If the shingles are wet, the wind is up, the light is going, or you would be working anywhere near power lines, stay on the ground. That covers almost every storm night.

What you can do from the ground: contain water inside, move vehicles away from damaged trees, and take your photos. A professional crew can get a tarp anchored properly without punching unnecessary holes in the roof, and a tarp done right buys weeks of protection while the real fix gets sorted out.

Frequently asked questions

Who should I call first after storm damage to my roof?

If there is a safety hazard like downed lines, a gas smell, or structural damage, call 911 or your utility first. Otherwise, call a local roofer to tarp any active leak and document the damage with photos. With dated photos and an honest damage assessment in hand, you can then decide whether filing an insurance claim makes sense.

Should I tarp my roof myself after a storm?

In most cases, no. Wet shingles, leftover wind, fading light, and steep pitches make storm-night roof work genuinely dangerous, and a fall costs far more than any leak. Contain the water from inside, take photos from the ground, and let a crew with the right equipment anchor the tarp properly.

Does homeowners insurance cover roof storm damage?

It depends on what caused the damage and what your policy covers, and no roofer can promise coverage. Sudden wind and hail damage is treated differently than gradual wear, which is why dated documentation matters so much. We inspect, photograph, and walk you through the claims paperwork, then you file the claim and your insurer decides what is covered.

What if the storm damage looks minor?

Get it inspected anyway, because hail bruising and broken shingle seals are nearly invisible from the ground and often do not leak until freeze-thaw works on them months later. A free inspection soon after the storm either gives you peace of mind or dated documentation while the damage is fresh, and both are worth the visit.
