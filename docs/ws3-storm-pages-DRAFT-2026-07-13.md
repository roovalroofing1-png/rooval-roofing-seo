<h2>What Hail Damage Looks Like on a Utah Roof</h2>
<p>Hail damage to asphalt shingles is physical, not just cosmetic — and most of the real signs only show up close. Here's what we check for on every slope.</p>

<h3>Bruises and soft spots</h3>
<p>A hailstone drives granules down into the asphalt mat and leaves a soft spot, like a bruise on an apple. You usually can't see it from the ground at all — our inspectors find bruises by hand, pressing gently along each slope. A bruised shingle looks fine today and leaks later.</p>

<h3>Granule loss</h3>
<p>The classic mark is a round or oval spot with a dark center — bare asphalt where the granules got knocked away — ringed by loose granules. Check your gutters and downspout splash zones after a storm: piles of granules that look like coarse black sand are a strong hint the roof took hits.</p>

<h3>Cracked shingle mat</h3>
<p>Bigger stones, or repeated hits in the same spot, can crack the fiberglass mat inside the shingle. That's functional damage — the waterproof layer itself is compromised. Industry research from IBHS suggests hail around 1 inch (quarter-size) is where visible impact marks generally start on standard shingles, with mat fracture more likely at 1.25 inches and up.</p>

<h3>Dented soft metals</h3>
<p>Vent caps, flashing, gutters, and downspouts dent easily and — unlike shingles — metal never weathers back. Dings in the soft metals are one of the most reliable markers of how big the hail was and how hard it hit, so we photograph them on every inspection.</p>

<h3>Different roofs, different marks</h3>
<p>Asphalt bruises, wood shakes split along the grain, and metal roofing dents. Whatever you've got up there, the inspection is the same: walk it, touch it, photograph it.</p>

<h2>Utah's Two Storm Threats: Hail and Wind</h2>

<h3>Monsoon-season hail (July–August)</h3>
<p>Utah's hail arrives with the summer monsoon — short, intense thunderstorms, mostly July through August, that can hammer one neighborhood and miss the next street over. NOAA's <a href="https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/stormevents/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NCEI Storm Events Database</a> is the public record of those events, logged by county and date.</p>

<h3>Canyon windstorms (fall and winter)</h3>
<p>The wind threat is different: downslope windstorms that pour out of the Wasatch canyons in fall and winter and hit hardest at roof edges, ridges, and corners. Both threats are covered in depth on the repair pages — this guide is about how we record what they leave behind.</p>

<h2>How Hail Damage Shows Up in an Inspection</h2>
<p>On a hail inspection, the job isn't just finding damage — it's recording it so you can see exactly what's there and where.</p>

<h3>A bruise, found by hand and marked</h3>
<p>Hail bruises rarely photograph well from a distance, so the inspector finds them by touch — a soft spot where granules were driven into the mat — then notes the slope and location so each photo can be placed on the roof.</p>

<h3>Granule loss, in close-up</h3>
<p>The camera goes in tight on each impact: a round mark with a dark exposed-asphalt center, ringed by displaced granules. A gutter shot showing granule wash-down backs it up.</p>

<h3>Cracks in the shingle mat</h3>
<p>Where an impact has fractured the fiberglass mat, we photograph the crack up close and note it as functional damage — the waterproof layer itself, not just the surface. Industry research from IBHS ties mat fracture mostly to hail 1.25 inches and larger.</p>

<h3>A dented vent as a size record</h3>
<p>Soft metals — vent caps, flashing, gutters — hold dents permanently, so they make a reliable record of how big the hail was. We photograph them alongside a reference for scale.</p>

<h2>How Wind Damage Shows Up in an Inspection</h2>
<p>Wind leaves subtler tracks than hail, so the recording method changes: more raking angles, more hands-on checks.</p>

<h3>A crease, photographed edge-on</h3>
<p>A wind crease is nearly invisible straight-on, so we shoot it low and edge-on, where the fold line catches the light, and note which slope and course it sits in.</p>

<h3>The broken-seal check</h3>
<p>The inspector gently tests bond lines by hand; a shingle that lifts freely has a failed seal strip. We photograph the lifted edge mid-check so the failure is visible, not just described.</p>

<h3>Edge and ridge overview shots</h3>
<p>Because uplift concentrates at ridges, rakes, and corners, every write-up includes wide shots of those zones — missing tabs, torn shingles, lifted flashing — so the pattern of damage reads at a glance.</p>

<h3>Exposed decking, flagged urgent</h3>

<h2>Why You Can't See Most of It From the Ground</h2>
<p>Bruises are found by touch. Creases show only at an angle. Broken seals show only when a hand tests the bond. A drive-by look — yours or a contractor's — catches almost none of it, which is why our inspections mean boots on every slope. And please don't climb up yourself: after a storm, shingles can be loose, decking can be soft, and no photo is worth a fall. That's what the free inspection is for.</p>
